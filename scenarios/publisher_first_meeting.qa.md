# Hypergryph Meeting — Possible Questions and Answers

## 1. About the Team

**Q. Can you tell us a little more about your team?**

Yes.
We are a very small team.
Right now, Concode is mainly two people.

I am Myungjin Shin.
I handle programming and the overall business side.

I have worked as a developer for about 17 years.
Before Concode, I worked at Naver and LINE.

Sunhyang Kim is our artist.
She has about 10 years of experience.
She also worked at LINE before.

We first met at LINE.
After that, we started Concode together in 2022.

Our first game was The Way Home: Pixel Roguelike.
It reached about 3 million downloads.

Now we are making Graytail.

---

**Q. You are only two people. How have you been able to make a game like Graytail?**

I think it is possible because our roles are very clear.

I focus on programming, game systems, and business.
Sunhyang focuses on art and visual direction.

Also, we both have long experience.
So we can make decisions quite fast.

Of course, being a small team has limits.
But it also helps us keep a clear vision.

For Graytail, we have spent more than three years building the core technology, art style, and game structure.

---

**Q. Do you plan to expand the team before release?**

For Graytail, one or two more people would be helpful.

But we are careful about growing too fast.
We do not want to scale up without a strong base.

If we grow the team, we want to do it step by step.
And we want to keep the core direction inside the team.

We may use outsourcing when needed.
But important game decisions should stay with us.

---

## 2. About The Way Home

**Q. What did you learn from The Way Home?**

The Way Home taught us that we can reach players globally, even as a small team.

It reached about 3 million downloads.
Most users came organically.

We did not spend a lot on marketing.
And the game did not have gacha.

So it was not a very aggressive monetization game.
But it still made about 900 million Korean won in revenue.

That gave us confidence.

We learned that if the game has charm, players can find it and share it.

---

**Q. Why do you think The Way Home worked well?**

I think it had a clear emotional tone.
It was simple to understand.
And it had a strong survival and exploration loop.

Also, the pixel art style helped.
Many players felt it was cute, warm, and easy to approach.

It was not a huge game.
But it had a clear identity.

That is also something we care about in Graytail.

---

## 3. About Graytail’s Potential

**Q. What makes you confident about Graytail?**

We have shown Graytail at exhibitions.
And the player response was very positive.

The demo feedback was also encouraging.

Many players liked the art style, the atmosphere, and the mystery of the story.
They also responded well to the puzzle and adventure structure.

Of course, the game is not finished yet.
There is still a lot of polishing to do.

But we feel the core appeal is already working.

That is why we believe Graytail has strong potential.

---

**Q. What still needs the most work before release?**

The biggest work is finishing the full content.

We need to complete the later chapters, bosses, puzzles, and story moments.
We also need more polishing.

For example, combat feel, pacing, guidance, and balance all need more work.

The demo helped us a lot.
It showed us what players enjoy, and also what we need to improve.

So from now on, our focus is execution and polish.

---

**Q. What is your target for Graytail’s launch?**

Our target release window is around the middle of next year.

Before release, we want to reach around 100,000 to 200,000 wishlists.

For sales, our first goal is at least 100,000 copies.

If Graytail sells more than 300,000 copies,
we would see that as a very strong result for us.

Of course, we want to aim higher.
But as a small team, that would already be a very meaningful success.

---

**Q. Which platforms are you planning for?**

We plan to release first on PC and consoles.

After that, we are also thinking about Nintendo Switch and mobile.

We are already designing the game with performance in mind.
We are also careful about interactions, because we may bring the game to mobile later.

But the first priority is PC and console.

---

## 4. About the Game Direction

**Q. What kind of experience do you want players to have with Graytail?**

We want players to feel like they are exploring a mysterious island.

At first, it should feel like an adventure.
The player is looking for someone.
They solve puzzles, explore places, and fight enemies.

But as the game continues, the story becomes more strange.
The player starts to question what is real.

So we want Graytail to feel like both an adventure game and a mystery story.

The player should enjoy the world.
But they should also feel that something is wrong under the surface.

---

**Q. The game has a strong visual style. How important is that to your strategy?**

It is very important.

We are a small team, so we need a strong identity.
The visual style is one of the biggest ways to show that identity.

With Graytail, we have built a 2.5D pixel-art style with HD-2D elements.
We use lighting, depth, water, fog, and 3D backgrounds to make the world feel rich.

We think this style can help the game stand out.

Also, we want to use this technology again in our future games, including The Way Home 2.

---

**Q. How do you balance puzzle, action, and story?**

We want all three parts to support each other.

The puzzles should make exploration more interesting.
The action should give tension and rhythm.
And the story should give the player a reason to keep going.

We do not want Graytail to be only a puzzle game.
And we do not want it to be only an action game.

We want it to feel like a complete adventure.

---

## 5. About the Story

**Q. Can you explain the story direction a little more?**

The story starts in a simple way.

Sophie asks the main character to find her mother.
So he goes to the island with her.

But during the journey, Sophie is kidnapped.
The player then tries to save Sophie and find the truth behind the island.

As the story goes on, the world becomes more strange.
The player starts to feel that the island is hiding something deeper.

In the end, the story is not only about saving Sophie.
It is also about the main character facing his own memories, guilt, and truth.

The structure is a little similar to Shutter Island.

---

**Q. Is the story already fully decided?**

The main structure is decided.

We know the beginning, the major twists, and the ending.
We also know the emotional direction.

Some details may still change during development.
For example, pacing, dialogue, and some chapter details may be adjusted.

But the core story is clear.

---

**Q. What is the ending about?**

There is a false ending, where it looks like the main character defeats Salvatore and saves Sophie.

But the true ending reveals a deeper truth.

The island is connected to virtual reality.
And the main character has to face the truth that he lost his daughter Sophie because of his own mistake.

So the ending is not only about defeating a villain.
It is about whether the main character can accept the truth or not.

---

## 6. About Funding and Publishing

**Q. What kind of partnership are you looking for right now?**

Right now, our first priority is publishing or project-based funding.

We have spent a long time making Graytail.
So we need funding to finish development in a stable way.

But we are not only looking for money.

We want a partner who understands the game.
A partner who can help with marketing, platform strategy, and global release.

We want to finish the game well, and give it the best chance to succeed.

---

**Q. Are you open to equity investment?**

We are not completely against equity investment.

But right now, our first priority is Graytail.
We want to finish this game and make it successful.

So publishing or project funding feels closer to our current needs.

That said, if there is a good long-term vision,
we are open to discussing equity investment too.

We just want to be careful.
We do not want to scale up too fast without a clear reason.

---

**Q. Are you talking with other publishers or investors?**

Yes.
We are talking with some publishers, including Chucklefish.

But we have not started detailed contract discussions yet.

We also sometimes talk with the investment team at Krafton.
They have suggested that we consider investment.

But nothing is decided yet.

We are still trying to understand what kind of partner would be the best fit for us.

---

**Q. What would you like to know from Hypergryph?**

We would like to know what options Hypergryph has.

For example, publishing, project funding, equity investment, or other forms of partnership.

We are also curious about what kind of teams you are looking for.

And if you invest in a team,
we would like to understand how you usually work with that team after investment.

---

## 7. About Equity Investment

**Q. If this became an equity investment discussion, what would you want to understand?**

First, we would like to understand the structure.

For example, is it usually a seed investment?
What percentage do you usually consider?
How involved do you become after investment?

We also heard that Hypergryph and Krafton invested in Studio Sai together.

We are curious if that case was similar.
And whether that investment was more focused on scaling up the team.

We do not need an exact answer about that deal, of course.
But we would like to understand your general investment style.

---

**Q. Why are you cautious about equity investment?**

Because equity investment can change the direction of a studio.

It can be very helpful.
It can give stability and more opportunities.

But it can also create pressure to grow bigger and faster.

We are not against growth.
But we want to grow in a way that fits us.

Our goal is not to become a very large company.
Our goal is to make a small number of very good games.

So we want to be careful about the type of partnership we choose.

---

## 8. About the Future of the Studio

**Q. What kind of studio do you want Concode to become?**

We do not want to become a very large company.

And we do not want to make many games at the same time.

We want to make a small number of games very well.

Our ideal model is close to Team Cherry.

Maybe we can become a little bigger than Team Cherry.
For example, a team that can work on two projects at the same time.

But the most important thing is not team size.

The most important thing is building a structure
where we can make the games we truly want to make,
with high quality.

---

**Q. What is your plan after Graytail?**

After Graytail, we want to make The Way Home 2.

We want to use the HD-2D technology we built during Graytail.

We are also thinking about adding extraction game elements.

But right now, we do not want to think too far ahead.

The first goal is Graytail.
We need to finish it well.
And we want to make it successful.

After that, The Way Home 2 can become the next step.

---

## 9. About Risks

**Q. What do you think is the biggest risk for Graytail?**

The biggest risk is execution.

The core appeal is there.
Players responded well to the demo.

But we still need to finish the full game at a high quality.

Because we are a small team, time and resources are limited.
So we need to choose carefully what to focus on.

That is why funding and the right partner can be very helpful.

---

**Q. What would funding change for you?**

Funding would give us stability.

It would help us focus on development until release.
It could also help us bring in one or two more people, or use outsourcing where needed.

It would also help with marketing and launch preparation.

The goal is not to suddenly make the team huge.

The goal is to finish Graytail properly
and give it the best chance in the market.

---

## 10. Closing

**Q. What would be the best outcome from this meeting?**

For us, the best outcome is to understand each other better.

We would like to know what Hypergryph is looking for.
And we would like to share what kind of team we are,
what kind of game we are making,
and what kind of future we are imagining.

If there is a good fit,
we would be very happy to continue the conversation.

We are still a small team.
But we believe Graytail has strong potential.

And we want to find a partner who can help us make it successful.
